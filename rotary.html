Young Women in Rotary:
A New Frontier in Mentorship

A cross-disciplinary research project proposing a collective group mentorship framework to improve the engagement and retention of young women in Rotary International.

Role Group Member — Significance, Timeline & Implementation Plan

01

Overview

Rotary International is one of the world's most recognised humanitarian organisations — 1.2M+ members across 35,000+ clubs in 200 countries, guided by the mission of "service above self." Yet beneath that global footprint sits a structural problem: Rotary is ageing out. In Australia, the average member age has reached 71, and with 14 members leaving every day, the organisation urgently needs to engage the next generation.

The retention gap is most acute among young women. Only 23% of Rotary members globally are female, and women leave at disproportionately high rates — with exit interviews citing sexism, lack of respect, and a culture not designed for them. This project was commissioned to examine why, and to propose a concrete, implementable solution.

Undertaken as part of a cross-disciplinary university research cohort, our team of six — Group Taupe — produced a 4,000-word report analysing the root causes of young women's disengagement and proposing a collective group mentorship framework to address it. My contributions spanned establishing the project's significance, structuring the implementation timeline, and co-authoring the actionable steps section.

02

The Problem

Rotary's retention challenge is not simply demographic drift — it's structural. The organisation's culture, meeting formats, and communication were built around a membership profile that no longer reflects the world it aims to serve. Three pressure points consistently emerge across the data.

01

Young women leave at the two-year mark

Despite a genuine "charitable impulse" (Bendapudi et al., 1996), young women disengage before finding their footing. The two-year exit point is consistent across districts, suggesting a structural — not personal — failure at the point of onboarding and early engagement.

02

Cultural and structural barriers deter participation

Women's exit interviews from Rotary record experiences of "sexism, lack of respect and humiliation" (Joint Membership Chairs D9500/D9520, 2024). A lack of female representation in leadership creates a self-reinforcing cycle: women don't see a path, so they don't commit. Potential members are discouraged before they even join.

03

Young women's needs aren't being met

Young women in Australia face higher rates of loneliness and anxiety than men (AIHW, 2023), and a non-linear career arc that creates confidence gaps (Women's Agenda, 2022). What they need — community, soft skills, career networking, a safe space — is precisely what Rotary's existing formats fail to provide. Traditional formal meetings don't offer the social connection or professional relevance young women are seeking.

"[Rotary is] no longer attracting the young business people and professionals who started it."

— Pulley, 2016
03

Research Design

The project used an agile design thinking approach, guided by the 4S method (Garette et al., 2018) — empathise, define, ideate, prototype. Because Rotary's problem is inherently social and variable, a traditional linear approach would have been inadequate. We supplemented this with the RADAR method (Pilkington, 2020) to structure our research-to-recommendation pipeline.

Research was triangulated across five question areas, drawing on peer-reviewed literature, government and NGO reports, industry publications, ABS demographic data, and media analysis.

Research Q1

What programs have NGOs and Rotary put in place to engage youth — and why are they falling short?

Research Q2

What reasons have women provided for not joining or for leaving Rotary?

Research Q3

What are the experiences, needs, and pressures of young women in Australia today?

Research Q4

How is Rotary perceived by the public — and what does its organisational identity communicate?

Research Q5

What activities have organisations used successfully to develop career skills and build community in young women?

04

Key Findings

Five research insights shaped our proposal. Together, they confirm that Rotary's problem is structural — not a lack of mission relevance, but a failure to translate that mission into an experience young women want to stay in.

Finding 1

Existing youth programs are short-term and structurally weak

Rotary's current youth initiatives — Interact Club (12–18), RYLA, Youth Exchange, New Generation Service Exchange — are awareness-generating but retention-weak. Short-term programs don't support tight-knit community building, and programs targeting 12–18 year olds fail to create a compelling bridge into adult Rotary membership given the organisation's high time commitments and cost.

Finding 2

Women leave because of culture, not mission

A lack of representation in membership and leadership makes women feel marginalised. This is compounded by an absence of career advancement pathways, an organisational culture experienced as exclusionary, and communication that doesn't resonate with early-career professionals. Many potential members remain unaware of what Rotary can offer — and those who do join, leave.

"Sexism, lack of respect and humiliation."

— Women's exit interviews from Rotary (Joint Membership Chairs D9500/D9520, 2024)
Finding 3

Young women need community, career skills, and belonging

Statistically, young women in Australia face higher rates of loneliness and anxiety than men, and are navigating increasingly non-linear career paths (AIHW, 2023; Women's Agenda, 2022). Female Rotary members have directly reported a desire for social events that align with women's interests — not just formal meetings. What they're looking for is "professional renewal for both mentee and mentor" in a safe, community-oriented setting (ACECQA, 2018).

Finding 4

Rotary's identity is admirable but its public perception is compromised

Rotary's stated values include diversity, equity, and inclusion — and their Four-Way Test acknowledges the need to challenge unconscious bias. But publicly broadcast incidents of sexist content (Seven News Riverina, 2021) have undermined trust with the very demographic they need to recruit. The aspiration exists; the execution hasn't caught up.

Finding 5

Mentorship and community-building activities drive retention

Career skills are built through internships, leadership training, mentoring, and applied real-world experience (Hart Research Associates, 2015). Community forms through membership, influence, reinforcement, and shared emotional connection (McMillan & Chavis, 1986). Mentorship structures that create peer relationships and skill transfer are proven to meet both needs simultaneously — making them the most viable intervention point for Rotary.

05

The Framework

Our proposal: replace Rotary's traditional dyadic (one-to-one) mentoring model with a collective group mentorship framework. Collective mentorship (Dahlberg & Byars-Winston, 2019) forms a network of multiple experienced mentors supporting multiple mentees — where mentees also act as peer mentors and reverse mentors.

In Rotary's context, this translates to a network of experienced Rotarians co-mentoring a cohort of newly joined women — matched by professional interest, meeting formally and informally, and building community through structured activities alongside traditional service work.

1

Addresses women's need for community and belonging

Collective mentorship enables peer socialisation, psychosocial support, and friendship formation — directly countering the intimidation of formal one-on-one mentoring and the alienation of traditional male-dominated meeting culture. It creates a comfortable, interpersonal environment where women feel a sense of belonging.

2

Delivers diverse career skills and professional development

A single mentor can't meet all a mentee's varied needs. A group of interdisciplinary mentors — across marketing, finance, healthcare, education, technology — offers a range of perspectives and expertise. Peer discussion mitigates groupthink; reverse mentoring creates a feedback loop where mentees also enrich mentors' development.

3

Low cost, low commitment, high scalability for Rotary

Given Rotary's constrained time and monetary resources, collective mentorship is efficient: fewer mentors needed, less pressure on individuals, and greater reach. It's affinity-based — women are matched with others who share their interests — making it naturally aligned with Rotary's existing values of diversity, equity, and inclusion.

06

Implementation Plan

Engagement and retention depend on programme quality at every stage (Ngozi & Edwinah, 2022). The implementation plan runs across five sequential stages — designed to be piloted at the chapter level without requiring top-down organisational mandate.

Stage
Actions & Strategies
Timeline

Creating a Structure

Design and test the programme structure before launch. Personalise materials, activities, and workshops around the needs of women participants. Without this foundation, rollout risks conflict and confusion (Corkindale, 2011).
~2 months

Recruitment

Reach women mentors and mentees via social media, email newsletters, and in-person meetings. Cost-effective methods that can reach a large audience quickly, with attention to hard-to-reach populations (Darko et al., 2022).
~2 months

Matching Mentors & Mentees

Use compatibility surveys and focus groups (Kano, 1984) to match by professional interest and personality. Mentors must develop group facilitation skills: encouraging participation, managing group dynamics, fostering cohesion, and setting group rituals (Kupersmidt et al., 2020).
~1 month

Setting Objectives

Appoint experienced Rotarians to oversee the programme. Both mentors and mentees conduct individual development planning (IDP) and team goal-setting sessions. Locke's goal-setting theory (1968) grounds this: clear, challenging, committed goals improve engagement and sustain motivation.
~0.5 months

Support & Feedback

Provide ongoing support — mental health check-ins, career guidance, networking opportunities, and skill development. Continuous feedback ensures participants' voices shape the programme's evolution (Wondim et al., 2021).
Ongoing

Reflection

This project was my first experience working on a cross-disciplinary research team tasked with delivering an actionable recommendation to a real organisation. The complexity wasn't technical — it was human. Getting six people with different academic lenses to synthesise into a coherent proposal required constant translation, structured debate, and genuine compromise.

My background in science — where deadlines, collaborative analysis, and evidence-based decision-making are baseline expectations — turned out to be directly transferable to this kind of research consulting work. The discipline of "cite the claim, design the test" maps surprisingly well onto strategy.

The most revealing part of the research was understanding how deeply structural the problem is. Rotary doesn't have a brand awareness problem with young women — it has a cultural experience problem. Awareness campaigns won't fix what the room communicates when you walk in.

The collective mentorship framework works because it doesn't ask Rotary to rebuild from scratch — it gives chapters a modular tool they can adopt at their own pace. Feasibility matters. A theoretically optimal solution that organisations can't actually implement is just another unread report.

If I were to extend this project, I'd push to pilot Tier 1 (recruitment + matching) in two or three willing chapters, then measure 12-month retention against a baseline. That's the data Rotary International actually needs to commit to broader roll-out.
